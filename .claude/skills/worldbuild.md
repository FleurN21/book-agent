# World Building

You are developing the setting and atmosphere for a YA mystery novel. World details are saved in `manuscript/world/`.

## When creating a new location or setting element:

Save to `manuscript/world/[location-slug].md` using this structure:

```markdown
# [Location Name]

## Overview
What is this place? Why does it matter to the story?

## Sensory Details
- **Sight:**
- **Sound:**
- **Smell:**
- **Feel/texture:**
- **Time of day it's most important:**

## Layout
Key spatial details that matter for the mystery (entrances, exits, hiding spots, sight lines, where someone could eavesdrop).

## Atmosphere
What mood does this place evoke? How does it change between day and night, or when something goes wrong?

## Story Function
- What scenes happen here?
- What clues are found here?
- What secrets does this place hold?

## Rules & Details
Any specific facts about this place that must stay consistent (hours of operation, who has keys, how far it is from other locations, etc.)
```

## Mystery-Specific World Building

For mystery novels, the setting is practically a character. Focus on:

- **Access and opportunity:** Who can get where, and when? This is how alibis work.
- **Hiding places:** Where could evidence be hidden? Where could someone go unseen?
- **Atmosphere as tension:** The setting should amplify unease. An empty hallway at night. A locked room. A forest path after dark.
- **Familiarity breeds suspicion:** Settings the protagonist knows well become unsettling when the mystery recontextualizes them.

## If the author wants to develop the overall world:

Create a `manuscript/world/overview.md` covering:
- The town/city/school -- name, vibe, size
- Social dynamics -- cliques, hierarchies, who has power
- Time period and season
- Any rules or constraints that matter (curfews, surveillance cameras, phone policies)
- The "normal" that gets disrupted by the crime

Always check existing character and outline files to ensure world details align with established story facts.
